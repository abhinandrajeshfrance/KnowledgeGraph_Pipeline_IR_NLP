===== PAGE 6 =====
Wikidata (compared with DBpedea)
• Wikidata Manually curated by humans and bots
• Facts are added, corrected, and discussed
• While DBpedia prioritizes coverage (automatically extracted), 
Wikidata prioritizes correctness.
• Dbpedia uses human-readable URIs, while Wikidata use 
numeric, language-independent IDs. So DBpedia URIs are 
intuitive, Wikidata IDs are stable and unambiguous
• Example

===== PAGE 7 =====
Wikidata ontology, schema and data quality 
(compared with DBpedea)
• Ontology and schema
• DBpedia Uses a lightweight ontology (dbo:), its Classes and 
properties are explicitly defined
• Wikidata is schema-light, property-centric. Its properties are entities 
themselves
• Data Quality
• Dbpedia: noisy due to automatic extraction; Incomplete or incorrect 
facts are possible; Ontology mismatches occur
• Wikidata: data quality is higher, validated by the community; There are 
explicit references to sources
• If correctness matters, prefer Wikidata.

The Linked Open Data
• Linked Open Data (LOD) is a method of publishing structured data so that 
it can be interlinked and become more useful through semantic queries.
• Core characteristics: Open access, Structured (RDF), Identifiable (URIs), 
Interlinked
• It uses HTTP URIs to name things, and provide useful information 
(SPARQL query). 
• RDF is the datamodel behind LOD. 
• URIs are global identifiers. 
• Linking is explicit.
• owl:sameAs (identity)
• skos:exactMatch
• rdfs:seeAlso

PREFIX wdt:  <http://www.wikidata.org/prop/direct/>
PREFIX wd:   <http://www.wikidata.org/entity/>
PREFIX dbo:  <http://dbpedia.org/ontology/>
PREFIX dbr:  <http://dbpedia.org/resource/>
SELECT ?pop ?coords
WHERE {

wd:Q90 wdt:P625 ?coords .
}

===== PAGE 25 =====
Data Integration and Normalization
• Data integration and normalization is a critical step in building a 
high-quality knowledge graph. 
• Identical entity often have variations
• e.g., "IBM," "International Business Machines," "IBM Corp."
• Objective: resolve these variations and map them to unique, 
canonical representations.
25

===== PAGE 26 =====
Entity Resolution / Linking
• Purpose: Merge different mentions of the same entity into a single 
canonical entity.
• Techniques:
• String Similarity & Matching: 
• Use similarity measures (like Levenshtein distance or cosine similarity on word 
embeddings) to identify similar entity mentions.
• Contextual Clues: 
• Analyze surrounding text or use features like entity type, part-of-speech tags, and syntactic 
context to disambiguate entities.
• Knowledge Base Mapping: 
• Use external knowledge bases (e.g., DBpedia, Wikidata, or Freebase) to link extracted 
entities to canonical identifiers.
• Tools like DBpedia Spotlight or TAGME can automate this mapping.

===== PAGE 28 =====
Integrate private knowledge base with open 
knowledge bases
• Integrate your private KB with open KBs are very helpful, by 
making your own knowledge contextualized, interoperable, 
and reusable.
• By linking to open KBs. Your entities gain rich semantic 
descriptions and external attributes become accessible. 
• Eg, in your private KB, there is an entity ex:ProjectX. It is indeed the 
city Paris. However, there is no much semantic information about it, just 
the capital of France.
• By adding ”ex:ProjectX owl:sameAs dbr:Paris .” as a fact, your private 
data is no longer semantically isolated!
• Always Link to Open facts rather than duplication!

Linking, not duplicating!
• Bad example: 
• The population and country are COPIED from DBpedia to your private 
KB
• Good Practice:
• DBpedia remains the authority and your KB 
• Why doing so?
• Avoid data inconsistency (e.g. when Dbpedia updates population, your
KB outdates and conflicts with duplicate)
• Preserve authority and provenance (your implicitly claim authority if you
duplicate)

===== PAGE 30 =====
Advantages of linking (not dupicating). 
• (Following last slide)
• Reduce Maintenance Cost and avoid semantic drift (updates are 
synchronized automatically)
• Enable reasoning and inference
• Control privacy and exposure (linking exposes only the identity, while 
duplicating risks leaking public structure into private data and blurs 
public/private boundary)
• Query Power increases (linking allows federate query across dataset, 
and multi-hop questions are possible. E.g., “Which internal offices are 
in capital cities of countries with GDP > X?” )
• Better AI and RAG behaviours30

===== PAGE 32 =====
How to integrate?
• Assume that you have already build your private knowledge, with ontology 
and private URI namespace. You can simply add them as additional triplet 
to an open KB. 
• But sometimes, the open KB is too large ( DBpedia, Wikidata and YAGO 
include the entire Wikipedia…)
• Just find the neighbourhood subgraph from the open KB
• Entity Linking: Convert your text strings into standard IDs (in the open KB)
• Subgraph Extraction: Query the Open KB to get the "neighbors" of your entities. 
This turns your 100 triplets into perhaps 2,000–5,000 triplets.
• Merge: Combine your proprietary triplets with the fetched public triplets.
• (You need to do this in your project)
